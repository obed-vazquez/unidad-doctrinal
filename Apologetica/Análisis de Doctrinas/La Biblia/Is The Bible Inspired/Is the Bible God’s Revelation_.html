  1. Several Sources[bk]
    In a nutshell, Jesus Christ is the center of authority for the Bible. It is His life, teachings, and actions that provide the foundation and legitimacy for the Scriptures. Through His words and deeds, Jesus affirmed the divine inspiration and authority of both the Old and New Testaments. By referencing and validating the Old Testament in His teachings and commissioning His disciples to spread His message, Jesus established the basis upon which we recognize the Bible as God's revelation. Therefore, the authority of the Bible is inherently tied to the person of Jesus Christ, making Him the central figure in understanding and affirming the Scriptures as divinely inspired.

  • Authority of the Old Testament.
    The authority of the Old Testament can be derived from the actions and teachings of Jesus Christ as recorded in the Gospels. In two key instances, Jesus explicitly validates the Old Testament scriptures, thereby affirming their authority:

  1. The Temptation in the Desert:
    When Jesus was tempted by Satan in the desert, He responded to each temptation by quoting Old Testament scriptures, specifically from Deuteronomy (Matthew 4:1-11). These references demonstrate that Jesus regarded these scriptures as the authoritative Word of God, powerful enough to counter Satan’s temptations. By citing these texts, Jesus not only resisted Satan but also affirmed the divine authority and reliability of the Old Testament.
  2. The Last Supper:
    During the Last Supper, Jesus and His disciples sang hymns, which are widely believed to have included the Psalms (Matthew 26:30; Mark 14:26). The Psalms, a significant part of the Old Testament, were thus endorsed by Jesus through their use in this solemn and pivotal moment. This act underscores that Jesus viewed these writings as sacred and authoritative.

Through these actions, Jesus Christ effectively endorses the specific portions of the Old Testament He referenced, and by logical extension the entire TaNajK (Old Testament), affirming its status as divinely inspired and authoritative scripture.

  • Authority of the New Testament.

The authority of the New Testament is rooted in the commission given by Jesus to His disciples and the apostolic teachings that followed. This authority is underpinned by several key considerations:

  1. Commission of the Disciples:

Jesus instructed His disciples to teach all that He had commanded them (Matthew 28:19-20). This mandate suggests that the teachings and writings of the apostles, as direct transmitters of Jesus’ instructions, carry His authority Hebrews 1:1-2 “He has spoken to us by His Son”. This verse highlights the transition from the Old Testament prophets to the teachings of Jesus and His apostles as the primary source of divine revelation.

Furthermore, 2 Timothy 3:16 states that all scripture is God-breathed. This can be understood to encompass the New Testament writings, as they were produced by those directly commissioned by Jesus. The context of this verse indicates that the author, Paul, is referring to his own writings and those of his fellow apostles. The passage suggests that these writings, inspired by the Holy Spirit, are part of the divine revelation and authoritative for the church.

  1. Scripture Harmony:

Paul is a unique case among the New Testament authors. Although he was not among the original twelve apostles, his encounter with the risen Christ on the road to Damascus and subsequent teachings were recognized and affirmed by the early church. Paul’s epistles were accepted as scripture by the apostles, including Peter, who equated Paul’s letters with other apostle scriptures.

To understand why Paul’s writings were accepted, we can consider the concept of tacit endorsement. In various contexts, including judicial and logical frameworks, silence or lack of refutation can imply consent or agreement. The apostles, by not refuting Paul’s teachings and letters, implicitly endorsed them. This principle is evident in how Peter refers to Paul’s writings. Peter not only refrains from refuting Paul but also acknowledges the wisdom given to Paul by God, placing his letters on the same level as other scriptures.

This should imply that the apostles were vigilant in addressing any disagreements or incorrect teachings/writings among themselves. An example of this that will serve as a validation is found in the case of the circumcision, where Paul confronted Peter publicly because Peter's actions were not in line with the truth of the gospel (Galatians 2:11-14). This incident demonstrates that if any apostle had an issue with the teachings or writings of another, they would promptly speak up and address it. The fact that Paul's teachings were not refuted by the other apostles, but rather accepted and even endorsed, reinforces the argument of tacit endorsement and validates the authority of his writings as divinely inspired scripture.

One can see not only a tacit endorsement but an explicit one when Peter writes in 2 Peter 3:15-16, recognizing Paul’s letters as part of the scriptural canon. Peter’s acknowledgment serves as a clear validation of Paul’s authority and the divine inspiration of his writings. The early church’s acceptance of Paul’s epistles, despite his unique background and direct revelation from Christ, highlights the consistency and unity of the apostolic message.

Understanding these tacit and explicit endorsements helps clarify why the early church viewed Paul’s writings as authoritative. The lack of objection from the other apostles and their recognition of Paul’s inspired teachings indicate a collective acknowledgment of his role and contributions to the New Testament canon. This principle not only applied to Paul’s epistles but also extended to other manuscripts[bl] the church was collecting over time. During the first two centuries, the early church carefully preserved and validated these writings, ensuring they conformed to the same pattern of acceptance and endorsement. This process demonstrates the collaborative and discerning nature of the early church in establishing the authoritative scriptures and contributed to the development of the New Testament canon as we know it today.
